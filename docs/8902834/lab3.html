The loss on the Training and Testing Datasets:

The loss in the training or testing dataset is a measure of how well the model fits the training or testing data. It quantifies the discrepancy between the actual target values (X_train)(y_train) and the predicted values generated by the model. The lower the loss, the better the model is at fitting the training or testing data. However, it is important to note that a low training or testing loss does not guarantee good performance on unseen data. It is essential to evaluate the model's performance on a separate test dataset to assess its generalization capability.
The loss on the training dataset is 3827.8186984113813
The loss on the testing dataset is 4150.6801893299835

Takeaways:

1. The model's slope (m) indicates the rate of change in disease progression per unit change in BMI. A positive value suggests that higher BMI is associated with increased disease progression.
2. The intercept (c) represents the estimated disease progression when BMI is zero. However, in the context of BMI, an intercept of zero is not meaningful since BMI cannot be zero in reality.
3. The loss on the test dataset provides an indication of how well the model generalizes to unseen data. A lower loss value indicates better performance but we have a high loss in training and testing dataset.

To conclude, the univariate linear regression model on the diabetes dataset provides a reasonable fit, suggesting that BMI may be a useful predictor for disease progression. However, further analysis and evaluation of additional features could provide a more comprehensive understanding of the factors influencing disease progression in diabetes. Using the remaining attributes we can train our model much better and get good results.
